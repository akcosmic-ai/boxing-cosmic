Ground Rules: Low-Impact by Design

Non-negotiable movement rules for this program. Everything here is built low-impact on purpose: joints last longer when pounding stays out of training, and old-school boxing footwork was flat and grounded anyway. You lose nothing by training this way.

  • No bouncing, no hopping, no jump rope. Feet glide, they never leave the floor. Imagine a sheet of paper under each foot.
  • Step-drag is your only travel method. Lead foot steps a few inches, rear foot drags to restore stance. Small steps, weight stays low and centered.
  • Pivot on the ball of the foot with weight unloaded. When you pivot for the rear hand, let the heel lift and rotate light. Never grind a loaded joint.
  • Pain is data, not weakness. Sharp or deep joint pain during or after a session means stop, shorten the round, or swap footwork rounds for pure hand-technique rounds that day.
  • Training around an injury or joint condition? Clear it with your doctor first. Tell them exactly what this involves: flat-footed shuffles, small steps, rotational punching.

Warm-Up · 5 Minutes, Before the Timer

  • 1 min: arm circles, shoulder rolls, wrist circles, neck rolls.
  • 1 min: hip circles and gentle leg swings within a comfortable range. Never force the range.
  • 2 min: slow shadowboxing at 50% speed. Just stance, guard, and lazy jabs. Grease the pattern, not the muscles.
  • 1 min: 10 slow bodyweight squats to comfortable depth plus torso rotations. Punching power comes from rotation, so wake it up.

Foundation Session · 3:00 Work / 1:00 Rest

Round 1Shadowbox + Movement (no bag)

Face a mirror or your shadow. Step-drag forward, back, left, right. Throw only the jab while moving. The goal is that your stance looks identical after every step: feet shoulder-width, rear heel light, hands at cheekbones.

  • Pattern: step forward + jab, step back + jab, circle left 2 steps, circle right 2 steps. Repeat.
  • Every 30 seconds: one slow slip left, one slow slip right (bend at knees and waist slightly, eyes stay on target).
Cue: if your feet ever cross or click together, freeze, reset stance, continue. That habit matters more than anything you do on the bag.
Watch the form · Step-drag (ExpertBoxing)
Round 2Jab Only

Entire round, nothing but jabs on the bag. This is what separates people who "hit the bag" from people who box. Vary it: single jab, double jab, jab to the body (bend knees, do not drop your hand).

  • Step-drag in to jabbing range, jab, step-drag out. Never stand still for more than 2 punches.
  • Snap it back faster than it went out. The bag should pop, not push.
Cue: exhale sharply ("tss") on every punch. Rotate the fist at the end. Return to your chin, not your chest.
Watch the form · Jab on the heavy bag (Final Round Training)
Round 3The 1-2 (Jab, Cross)

Add the rear hand. Power in the cross comes from the ground up: rear foot pivots, hip fires, shoulder follows, fist arrives last. If your arm is doing the work, you are doing it wrong.

  • First minute: slow 1-2 at 60% power, feel the hip rotation.
  • Second minute: 1-2 then step-drag out at an angle, not straight back.
  • Third minute: 1-1-2 and 1-2-1 mixes at 80%.
Cue: keep the pivot light on the ball of the foot. If pivoting bothers your rear hip or knee, shorten the pivot and get rotation from the torso instead.
Watch the form · The 1-2 (Tony Jeffries, Olympic medalist)
Round 4Defense Round

Treat the swinging bag as an opponent. Push the bag so it swings, then work off its movement. Defense at home is about making these reflexive.

  • As the bag swings toward you: step back out of range, or slip off-line to either side (small knee-and-waist bend, no lunging).
  • After every defensive move, answer with a punch: slip right then cross, step back then jab as it returns.
  • Mix in the catch: glove blocks an imaginary jab at your forehead, then counter.
Cue: a slip only needs to move your head the width of your head. Big dramatic slips are slower and load the hips more.
Watch the form · How to slip punches (FightCamp)
Round 5Combinations + Angles

Combine everything: punch, move, punch again from the new angle. This round builds the habit pros drill constantly, never admiring your work after a combo.

  • Core patterns: 1-2-3 (jab, cross, lead hook), 1-2 step-left 3, 1-2-3-2.
  • After every combination, take an angle: two step-drags left or right so the bag would have to turn to find you.
  • Add body shots: 1-2 to the head, 3 to the body (bend the knees, keep posture upright).
Cue: the hook comes from turning the lead hip and foot together, elbow up, arm at 90 degrees. Do not swing wide.
Watch the form · Lead hook on the heavy bag (Final Round Training)
Round 6Output Round

The conditioning round. Higher volume, but form is still the boss. The moment your punches turn into arm-pushes, drop to 70% and fix it.

  • Intervals: 20 seconds of continuous punching (straight punches, stay technical), 40 seconds of movement and jabs. Repeat 3 times.
  • Finish the last 20 seconds with everything you have, exhaling on every shot.
Cue: feet stay planted and flat during flurries. Speed comes from the hands and hips, never from bouncing.

Advanced Session · Unlocks in Phase 2

Same timer, harder script. Switch the session selector to Advanced once you can complete the Foundation session with clean form (typically weeks 5 and beyond). Turn on the voice coach here: reacting to called combos instead of pre-planning them is the closest solo substitute for a coach holding pads.

Round 1Footwork + Pivots (no bag)

Everything from Foundation Round 1, plus pivots. The pivot is how you face a moving opponent without crossing your feet, and how you exit after combinations.

  • Step-drag in all four directions, then pivot 45 to 90 degrees off the lead foot, both directions.
  • Pattern: jab, pivot left, jab, pivot right. Reset stance every time.
Cue: pivot light: heel up, weight through the ball of the lead foot, rear foot swings like a gate.
Watch the form · How to pivot (Tony Jeffries)
Round 2Jab Variations + Feints

The jab becomes a toolset: range-finder, power jab stepping in, up-jab from low guard, body jab, and the feint. A feint is a jab you show but never throw, sold with the shoulder and front knee.

  • Cycle: single, double, body jab, feint-then-jab, feint-then-cross.
  • Every feint must look identical to your real jab for the first 6 inches.
Cue: if the bag were a person, your feint should make them flinch. Sell it with the whole body, not the hand.
Round 3Uppercuts in Combination

The uppercut is a short-range punch thrown from the legs: dip the same-side knee slightly, then drive up through the hip. On the bag, aim where the chin would be, and keep it tight, not a swing from the floor.

  • First minute: slow rear uppercuts (5) and lead uppercuts (6) in isolation.
  • Then combinations: 1-2-5, 2-5-2, 1-6-3, 6-3-2.
Cue: elbow stays in front of your body, palm turns to face you. If your elbow flares behind you, it is a swing, not an uppercut.
Watch the form · Lead and rear uppercut in slow motion (FightCamp)
Round 4Slip-Roll-Counter Chains

Chain defenses together and always end with a counter. The roll (ducking under an imaginary hook by bending the knees and tracing a U with your head) is your new tool. Keep rolls small and knee-driven, never back-driven.

  • Chains: slip left, slip right, 2. Catch, roll, 3. Slip right, roll left, 2-3.
  • Push the bag and work all chains off its swing.
Cue: eyes stay on the bag through every roll. If you look at the floor, you are diving, not rolling.
Watch the form · How to roll a punch (Tony Jeffries)
Round 5Combos, Exits + the Check Hook

Every combination now ends with an exit: an angle step or a pivot. Then add boxing's great counter to an aggressive opponent, the check hook: a lead hook thrown while pivoting away from the pressure.

  • Patterns: 1-2-3 pivot out. 1-2-5-2 angle left. 2-3-2 angle right.
  • Check hook drill: push the bag hard so it swings at you, throw the 3 while pivoting your rear foot away. The bag should sail past where you were.
Cue: the check hook is timing, not power. Pivot and punch happen together, like a matador's turn.
Watch the form · Mastering the check hook (Barry Robinson)
Round 6Reaction Round

Turn the voice coach ON for this round. It calls combos and defensive commands at random intervals; execute instantly, then return to movement. No planning allowed. This is the round that builds fight reflexes.

  • Between calls: constant step-drag movement and jabs.
  • On a defensive call (slip, roll): execute it and always counter after.
Cue: speed of reaction beats speed of hand. A half-second late but committed beats instant and hesitant.

Self-Defense · What Transfers and What Doesn't

Boxing is one of the highest-value bases for real self-defense: distance control, timing, a trained jab and cross, and a calm head under pressure. But a street situation is not a boxing match. These are the rules that matter more than any punch.

  • The best win is the exit. Awareness, distance, and leaving early beat every technique on this page. Fighting is the last resort when escape is not available.
  • Manage distance with your feet and your jab hand. A raised open lead hand ("stop, back up") doubles as your range-finder and looks defensive to witnesses and cameras, because it is.
  • Bare knuckles break. Without wraps and gloves, straight punches to the skull crack hands. Palm-heel strikes hit almost as hard with far less risk. Train them on the bag occasionally: same mechanics as the cross, contact with the heel of the palm.
  • Never go to the ground by choice, and never stay. Your footwork training is your anti-takedown base: low, wide, feet never crossed.
  • One committed exit beats a combination. In a real situation the goal of any strike is to create the half-second you need to leave, not to win rounds.
  • Know your local self-defense laws. Proportional response and duty-to-retreat rules vary. A trained person is held to a higher standard.

12-Week Progression

Same six-round skeleton every session. You level up what happens inside the rounds, not the length of the workout. The Progress tracker above moves your phase automatically.

PhaseSessionFocusYou graduate when
Phase 1
Foundation		 Foundation session, voice coach off. Daily task every day. Stance, step-drag, jab, 1-2, small slips. Film yourself weekly and compare against the videos. Stance survives 3 minutes of movement without crossing feet; jab snaps back on its own. (~10 sessions)
Phase 2
Connection		 Alternate Foundation and Advanced. Voice coach on for 2 rounds. Feints, uppercuts, rolls, pivots, exits after every combo. You can throw a 1-2, exit on an angle, and be back in stance without thinking. (~20 sessions)
Phase 3
Fluidity		 Advanced session, voice coach on throughout. Check hooks, defense chains ending in counters, freestyle rounds, tempo changes (light-light-HARD). Someone watching cannot predict your rhythm. Then go get supervised pad/sparring work monthly.

Form Checkpoints (read before every session)

  • Power is rotational, not muscular. Ground, hip, shoulder, fist. In that order, every punch.
  • The bag should crack, not swing wildly. A big push means you're pushing, not punching. Snap and retract.
  • Hands return to your face, elbows stay tucked. Every punch you throw leaves a hole; close it fast.
  • Breathe out on every punch. Holding your breath kills your gas tank and your power.
  • Chin down, eyes up. Look at the bag through your eyebrows.
  • Wrist straight on impact. Land with the first two knuckles. Wraps under your gloves are worth it.
