Technique Guide

Every technique in the program, drawn with the form points marked exactly where they happen on the body. Target is always to the RIGHT of the figure. Orthodox stance (left foot forward); mirror everything if you're southpaw.

The number system the voice coach uses. Every punch shares one engine: ground, hip, shoulder, fist, in that order. The arm is the delivery, never the source.

1The Jab
  • Push straight from the shoulder; the fist rotates palm-down in the last 6 inches.
  • Land with the index and middle knuckles, wrist locked straight.
  • Exhale sharply on impact. The return is part of the punch.
  • Optional small step-in with the lead foot makes it a power jab.
Most common mistake: dropping the hand on the way back. It returns to your cheekbone on the same line it went out.
Watch: Jab on the heavy bag (Final Round Training)
2The Cross
  • The rear foot pivot starts everything: heel lifts, knee turns in, hip fires through.
  • Pull the lead shoulder back as the rear shoulder drives forward; that torque is the power.
  • Weight stays centered. If you fall forward onto the punch, you pushed it.
  • Chin stays glued behind your punching shoulder for protection.
Most common mistake: punching with the arm and leaving the rear heel flat. No pivot means no power, roughly 75% of a cross comes from legs and trunk.
Watch: The 1-2 (Tony Jeffries)
3The Lead Hook
  • Lift the elbow to shoulder height and lock the arm at 90 degrees; it stays frozen while the body turns.
  • The lead foot pivots inward (toes turn toward your rear foot) as the hip whips around.
  • Short arc. The punch travels maybe 12 inches; the rotation does the damage.
  • Best thrown after the 2 has turned your body the other way, like winding a spring.
Most common mistake: swinging wide with a loose arm. If your elbow trails behind your fist, it's an arm slap, not a hook.
Watch: Lead hook on the heavy bag (Final Round Training)
4The Rear Hook
  • Same engine as the cross: rear foot pivots, rear hip drives; the arm is just bent instead of straight.
  • A short-range punch. If you're at jab distance, throw the 2 instead.
  • Lands beautifully after a 3 has turned their guard, or to the body under their elbow.
Most common mistake: winding it up behind your head. Any punch you load backwards is visible a week in advance.
Watch: Heavy bag fundamentals (Tom Yankello)
5The Rear Uppercut
  • Dip the knees slightly and drop the rear hand a few inches, then drive up through the rear hip.
  • The fist travels a short vertical line to where the chin would be. Not a bowling swing from the floor.
  • Elbow stays in front of your ribs the whole way; palm rotates to face you at impact.
  • Combos: 1-2-5, 2-5-2, and 5 to the body when you're inside.
Most common mistake: pulling the hand down to your waist first. The dip comes from the knees, never from dropping your guard.
Watch: Both uppercuts in slow motion (FightCamp)
6The Lead Uppercut
  • Smaller and faster than the 5. A little knee dip, lead hip drives up, fist pops up the middle.
  • Deadly at close range, especially right after slipping to your lead side.
  • Classic chains: 6-3 (uppercut lifts the chin, hook meets it) and 1-6-3.
Most common mistake: leaning back to make room. Stay over your feet; if you need room, step, don't lean.
Watch: Both uppercuts in slow motion (FightCamp)

Defense wins fights and self-defense situations alike. Rule for all of these: every defensive move ends loaded to counter, or it was wasted. Red line = the incoming punch.

D1The Guard (home base)
  • Everything launches from here and returns here. Punches are round trips.
  • Forearms cover the body's centerline; you see the target through your eyebrows.
  • Shoulders stay relaxed. A tense guard is slow and burns your gas tank.
Most common mistake: guard drifting down as you get tired. When your gloves touch your cheekbones, you feel it and fix it instantly.
D2The Catch (block the jab)
  • Rear glove opens slightly and meets their jab a few inches in front of your face, like catching a ball.
  • Don't reach for it. Let it arrive; reaching opens your chin for the cross behind it.
  • Instant counter: catch and jab back at the same time. Their arm is out; their face is open.
Most common mistake: slapping at the punch. The glove barely moves; the catch is firm, not a swat.
Watch: How to defend any punch (Tony Jeffries)
D3The Parry
  • As their jab arrives, your rear hand taps it sideways-down, 2 or 3 inches, no more.
  • Your hand deflects; their momentum does the rest. They're now turned and open.
  • Best counter: parry and throw your own jab or cross straight down the middle.
Most common mistake: big swatting motion that pulls your own hand off your face. If your glove leaves your head to parry, you traded defense for defense.
D4The Slip
  • Head moves one head-width off the centerline. That's it. The punch misses by an inch and you're still in range to counter.
  • Small bend at the knees and waist together; the spine stays fairly tall.
  • Slip OUTSIDE their jab (to your lead side) when possible; it puts you on their blind side.
  • Every slip loads a counter: slip right loads your 2, slip left loads your 3 and 6.
Most common mistake: big lunging slips with the eyes dropping to the floor. If you can't counter from where your slip ends, it was too big.
Watch: How to slip punches (FightCamp)
D5The Pull / Step Back
  • Two versions: the pull (weight shifts back, feet stay) and the step back (full step-drag out of range).
  • The pull makes them miss by an inch, keeping you close enough to counter instantly as they fall short.
  • The moment their punch dies, snap forward behind your own 2. The pull-counter-2 is a classic knockout.
Most common mistake: leaning straight back with the feet square and staying there. Off-balance and blind. Pull, then immediately return fire or angle out.
D6The Roll (under the hook)
  • Bend the knees to drop your head under the incoming hook, shift weight across, rise on the other side.
  • Eyes stay on the opponent the whole way. Looking at the floor is diving, not rolling.
  • You surface on the outside of their punch with your own 3 loaded for free.
Most common mistake: bending at the waist with straight legs. Your head drops but your chin points at the floor and your back does the work. Squat, don't bow.
Watch: How to roll a punch (Tony Jeffries)

This program's movement is deliberately flat and grounded: feet glide, never hop. That's not a limitation, it's how the old masters moved. Top-down views show the feet; red = start, green = finish.

M1The Stance
  • Feet shoulder-width, lead toe and rear heel roughly on one line, both feet angled about 45 degrees toward the target.
  • Knees soft, weight even. From here you can hit, defend, or move in any direction without adjusting first.
  • Test: have someone shove your chest lightly. A real stance absorbs it; a fake one staggers.
Most common mistake: standing too square (both shoulders facing the target). Blade slightly; you're a smaller target and the 2 loads itself.
M2Step-Drag (top-down)
  • Moving toward the target: lead foot steps first, rear drags. Moving away: rear steps first, lead drags. The foot nearest your direction always moves first.
  • Steps of a few inches. Stance width never changes; feet never cross, never click together.
  • Feet skim the floor like there's paper under them. Zero bounce.
Most common mistake: a big first step that leaves you long and off-balance for a half second. That half second is when you get hit.
Watch: Step-drag (ExpertBoxing)
M3The Pivot (top-down)
  • Weight rolls onto the ball of the lead foot; the rear foot sweeps 45 to 90 degrees around it.
  • Hands stay up and you stay facing the target the entire time; only your position changes.
  • Use it to exit after combos, to face a circling opponent, and to power the check hook.
Most common mistake: pivoting on a flat, planted foot. Heel up, weight light, or the knee takes the twist.
Watch: How to pivot (Tony Jeffries)
M4Angling Out (top-down)
  • The instant your last punch lands, take two quick step-drags at 45 degrees, left or right.
  • The opponent must turn to find you. During that turn they can't punch and you can.
  • On the bag: punch, angle out, and re-attack from the new line before the bag swings back.
Most common mistake: stepping straight back instead. Straight back keeps you on their tracks; they follow and land. Off the line, they have to reset.

These separate advanced fighters from strong beginners. Earn them: they only work when the basics underneath are automatic.

H1The Feint
  • A punch you show but don't throw. The first 6 inches must be identical to your real jab: same shoulder, same knee, same eyes.
  • Watch what their body answers with. Flinch high? The body is open. Parry hand drops? Jab is home free.
  • Rhythm: feint, read, punish. Feint-2, feint-body jab, and double-feint-3 are the classics.
Most common mistake: lazy half-hearted feints. If it wouldn't scare YOU, it won't move them. Sell every one like it's real.
H2The Check Hook
  • Against an opponent charging forward: throw the lead hook WHILE pivoting your rear foot away.
  • Their momentum walks them into the punch and past where you were standing. A matador's move.
  • Drill it on the bag: push it hard so it swings at you, hook + pivot as it arrives. The bag sails through empty space.
Most common mistake: hooking first, pivoting second. They happen in the same beat or you get run over.
Watch: Mastering the check hook (Barry Robinson)
H3The Shoulder Roll
  • Blade your body, drop the lead hand low, and raise the lead shoulder to your chin. Their cross glances off the shoulder.
  • As the punch slides off, your body is already rotated to fire the counter 2. Defense and offense in one motion.
  • The Mayweather/Philly shell signature. Drill it slowly against the swinging bag before trusting it.
Most common mistake: using it before the basics are automatic. This is a timing weapon for Phase 3, not a beginner guard.
H4The Cross Counter (slip + 2)
  • Slip their jab to your lead side and fire the 2 over the top of their extended arm in the same beat.
  • Their own jab blocks their vision; your cross arrives on the blind line. The oldest knockout in boxing.
  • Solo drill: voice coach says "slip", you slip and IMMEDIATELY throw the 2. Wire them together.
Most common mistake: slip, pause, then punch. The slip IS the wind-up; counter inside the same second or the window closes.
H5The Body Jab
  • Bend the KNEES to change levels, keep the back fairly tall, and spear the jab to where the solar plexus would be.
  • Your rear hand stays extra tight; your head is closer to their counters down there.
  • The payoff: after two body jabs, their guard drips down and your head shots walk in free.
Most common mistake: bowing at the waist with the head diving forward. That's a free uppercut for them. Knees down, head back.
H6Tempo Changes (no diagram needed)
  • Fighters get timed when everything is the same speed. Break your own rhythm: light-light-HARD, slow feint then explosive 1-2, long pause then a burst.
  • On the bag: throw two touch punches at 40%, then detonate the third at 95%. The third punch is the one that lands in real life.
  • Same trick with feet: two lazy step-drags, then one explosive angle-out. Predictable rhythm is the last beginner habit to die.
  • Listen for it in the voice coach reaction round: react at FULL speed to slow, randomly-timed calls. That gap between calm and explosion is the skill.
Most common mistake: training everything at one medium pace. Medium never hurt anyone. Train touches and train bombs; delete medium.
